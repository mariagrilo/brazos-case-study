Business//

The CTO team they didn't have

Good business, real clients, but the tech was a mess. Third-party tools duct-taped together. Spreadsheets everywhere.

His pitch: they know aviation, you know product. Come in and take over tech.

The pitch was: these guys know the business, you know tech and product. In terms of tech and product, you call the shots.

- João Ferreira (CPO)

The pivot//

Reports weren't the problem

After 2 or 3 interviews, we immediately shifted. They use reports a lot, but their users mostly go through the mundane things.

- João Ferreira

Day one, everyone agreed: build a reporting tool. The client was sure. They wanted charts, dashboards, something to replace PowerBI. We started scoping it out.

Then we talked to actual users. Two, maybe three interviews in, and the whole thing flipped. These flight analysts weren't making reports. They were reviewing events. Unsafe speeds, bad altitudes, weird angles. Dozens of them, every single day.

Their system? Open ten browser tabs. Check each one. Close them. Open ten more.

Reports? Once a month, maybe. The real work was this repetitive, tedious event review. Nobody had thought to mention it.

Politics//

It's someone's baby

Even if you know tech, you know product—this is still their baby. Tread lightly.

- João Ferreira (CPO)

Design//

The ten-tab problem

The ten-tab thing wasn't just inefficient. It was a symptom. Users opened browser tabs because that's how they tracked their to-do list. There was literally no other way.

So we built tabs into the app. Same mental model, but it actually works now. Queue up events, see the details on the side, flip through without losing your place.

It's not clever. It's obvious—if you've watched someone work for an hour. That's the whole point. We spent weeks just watching, asking questions, understanding the weird edge cases. You can't design something useful if you don't understand the mess it's replacing.

Same idea, better execution. The tab system lives inside the platform now.

The main challenge was understanding all the complex processes behind flight safety monitoring, and finding ways to simplify them from beginning to end.

- Maria Grilo (Product Designer)

Technical//

Half the team on one problem

Half the team on data visualization. And with our team size, half the team literally meant one developer.

- Pedro Oliveira (Lead Dev)

Flight data adds up fast. Thousands of events, complicated filters, different time ranges. If it's not instant, people won't use it. We built the whole data layer around that constraint.

The flight data provider gives us read access. Not write. A year in, this still causes problems. Users can't update event status through our platform—we found that out mid-build. So we focused on what we could control. The reporting and analysis still works, even without write-back.

Users can replay flights in 3D to see what happened. This existed before us, but it needed work. The zoomed-out view (all flights) and the zoomed-in view (one specific moment) both had to feel smooth.

HOW IT WORKS//

Three things, one platform

The platform does three jobs: see what's happening, dig into specific events, and share findings.

Each one is designed for a different speed of work.

Consultants and clients use the same platform but see different screens. Comments, notifications, flags - all in one place. No more email chains trying to figure out which flight had which problem.

REPORTS//

The thing they originally asked for

We did build reports. Just not first. By the time we got there, we understood who actually uses them, how often, and what they need.

The Brazos team made templates for common cases. Users can also build their own. Dynamic data, consistent formatting, none of the manual chart-wrangling that Power BI needed. Reports went from "the main feature" to "a thing that supports the real work."

The reporting was also pretty cool when we got to it. What type of reports, what customization do we need to empower them to have.

- Maria Grilo (Product Designer)

Results

They figured out what our users needed—sometimes before we did. They didn't build what we asked for. They built what actually made sense.

- Brazos Team

What we built

A flight safety platform that replaces a pile of disconnected tools. Event monitoring, investigation, reporting. One place. Used by teams working with ExxonMobil, J&J, Pfizer.

What changed

A company that knew aviation but not their users now talks to those users regularly. The shift from "we need reports" to "our people review events all day" changed how they think about priorities.

What's next

We're building a new system to receive data directly from aircraft. High availability, better scale. The goal: stop depending on third-party providers entirely.
